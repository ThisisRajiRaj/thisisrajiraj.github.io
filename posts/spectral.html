--- layout: post title: Astrophotography lessons date: December 24, 2021 ---

Couple of weeks ago, I did something I was very proud of. I learned a new skill - taking pictures of night skies flecked with trillions of stars.

This is not a trivial exercise for various reasons. For one thing, you need to have the right equipment: a camera with the appropriate aperture and shutter speeds, and a tripod. For another, it is best to have some experience with manual photography. But most importantly, you need to be at the right location. A place far enough away from civilization so light pollution doesn't tamper with your images.

I finally had all these pre-requisites ticked off this month.

Despite being a landscape photographer since 2009, it was only recently that I got a tripod. In the second week of December, we traveled to Joshua Tree National Park and a town called Julian in Southern California. These places boasted crystal-clear skies not touched by the lights of their surrounding communities.

My first night in Julian, after dinner, we drove through a valley that was as quiet as it was dark. There was minimal traffic. When I rolled down the window and poked my head out, I felt a calming breeze. I tilted my head up and saw a veritable fÃªte of stars above. I *had* to try my hand at taking pictures of that all that glory. We pulled over, I mounted my camera on my tripod, and pointed the lens up. I knew I had to have a long exposure to capture as much of the light from the stars as I could. In my naivete, I simply assume that slower was better and picked the slowest shutter speed I had on my camera outside of BULB. I took several pictures.



When I came back to the hotel and downloaded the images to my PC, though, I was disappointed. I could see that my images were uninspiring, and my composition was off. There was no reference point - like the silhouette of a mountain range or a tree in the foreground - that could make the images interesting and meaningful. Instead, my images were indistinguishable from an arbitrary black canvas with white specks on it.

When I went back out the next night again to take more star pictures, I knew I had to tweak my composition. My images needed a beginning, a middle, and an end; a subject that the viewer's mind could fathom because it had some context around it.

My subject was, of course, the infinity of the starlit sky, but what would give it meaning was the lonely valley I was standing in. Once the sky grew sufficiently dark, I started looking for foreground objects that could tell such a story. I experimented with different angles and different foreground interests until I got a few shots that looked just right on the camera viewfinder.

When I looked at these images again in the quiet and leisure of my hotel room, I was once again dissatisfied. The stars didn't look like blazing pinpricks on an ink-black sky. My camera was sensitive to the minutest light pollution in the valley and had captured that noise. My images looked hazy. The stars appeared shaken, thanks to the trails created by the rotation of the earth. My imagination could correct the flaws in my composition, but I now needed some serious technical knowledge of Astrophotography. I read online on how I should set up my camera for best results. I learned about the Rule of 500, the effect of using different drive modes on the sharpness of the image, and what ISO might be ideal.



The next night, before going out, I changed the settings on my camera based on what I had learned. I put my camera in manual shooting mode, set the white balance to auto and the shutter speed to between 15 and 18 seconds. With the largest aperture on my camera (f2.8), I adjusted the ISO to get the right exposure. I changed the drive mode to a 10-second timer to avoid unintended shakes. Out in the same secluded stretch of the road again, I mounted my camera, a mountain ridge in the foreground, and went to town on the shutter release.

Finally, my efforts yielded a few good pictures. Here is one of them. It is still not perfect, but a much improved version - I feel - from where I began. I now have a much better starting point to improve my star photography skills.



But more than good pictures, there were some valuable lessons in my expeditions.

  1. Behind every work that appears good - a well-written novel, an impeccable picture, a wonderful design - there are many sh*tty drafts. Lots of times, artists do not share their sh*tty drafts with the world, but they exist and are a prerequisite for more refined work.
  2. At the end of my three days of tinkering, I only got two or three images that I thought worked well. Patience and a strong stomach for "failure" are indeed virtues in anything worth learning and pursuing. I could have given up after my second attempt, but persisting meant that I gave myself the opportunity to improve and create something that I now cherish.
  3. Imagination helps you bring your signature to your work and solve problems that have never been solved before. But leveraging exsiting knowledge is just as important. While I used my imagination to tinker with my storytelling, the knowledge about the right shutter speeds was key to getting good images on my attempt #3. Granted, I could arrived at all of that knowledge by myself, but why not "stand on the shoulders of giants that have come before us", those that have uncovered vital knowledge already?

The mathematician-philosopher, Bertrand Russell, once said that "The good life is one inspired by love and guided by knowledge". Einstein argued that imagination is more important than knowledge. I find that both imaginaion and knowledge to needed to learn anything new and solve problems. A good life is inspired by love, guided by knowledge, but ignited by imagination.
