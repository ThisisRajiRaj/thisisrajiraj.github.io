Ok, confession time. I have not told this story to many people, so as I type these words, I am a bit nervous. This happened a long time ago. I tell you this story today because I think there are some lessons in it worth sharing.

Let me take you back to a time when I was a teenager. I lived with my father and my grandmother in a village in Tamil Nadu. It was a small village, and we all knew each other. One hot summer weekend that year, news came to us that an acquaintance - a man who lived in a different town - had started producing miracles. Yes, you heard that right. This man, a human who went to work and ate from stainless steel plates like the rest of us, had started performing miracles for his community. He had started worshipping a godman - a Guru - a few years back. With years of prayer and piety, he now supposedly had supernatural powers. Holy Ash was materializing out of the paintings of various gods in his prayer room. Ash that had miraculous healing properties. Crowds thronged to his house. People started falling at his feet, heck, worshipping him.

When the news of our acquaintance's miracle came to us, my father heard it and moved on. My grandma did the same. I, however, was disturbed. I had always let skepticism and curiosity whisper in my head like a hissing snake and make me do things that I'd later be scolded for. This time was no different. How could Holy Ash appear out of thin air, I pondered. Was it even real or supernatural? Is it possible that our acquaintance was making it happen? A part of my mind answered yes. I wanted to test the theory. I wanted to prove - or disprove - to myself that someone can engineer a miracle that many people will come to believe in.


I had a plan.

The next day, I woke up at 4AM, when the rest of the house slept. I padded over in the inky darkness of the house to our prayer room. Every Indian household I knew had a prayer room, complete with a supply of Holy Ash and Vermillion that we used as talismans daily. I entered ours. The room smelt of old oil, rotting flowers, and burnt incense. I opened our Holy Ash container, took a generous pinch of it, and splashed it on the painting of Lord Ganesha. I then opened the container of Vermillion, and splashed it on the picture of Goddess Durga, the goddess of courage. I surveyed my handiwork with satisfaction and left the room quietly.





The household stirred after an hour. The first person to notice the "miracle" in our prayer room was my grandma. She ran out of the room and dragged me in. She pointed to the ash and the vermilion. "Do you see it?" she asked, "God is visiting us too." She was genuinely moved.

Before the sun was high in the sky, and the worst of the heat started, the entire neighborhood had heard of our miracle. That evening, there was a line of people waiting to see the Holy Ash. Their reverence for my grandma became worship. My grandma was over the moon, not just by the godly validation of her religiosity, but by the attention and status it brought her. As I watched the procession of people into my house that evening, with my homework notebook open on my lap, I knew I had to continue my experiment. Fake ash can fool people once, but can it fool them again? I had to find out.


Over the next week, I continued my experiment.

I continued sprinkling Holy Ash and Vermilion, making people believe that our house was specially blessed by the gods. No one seemed to question the veracity of it. I realized that I could continue this, possibly for a long time. That was when the guilt began. Having validated my theory, the thrill of the experiment subsided. I started to worry that what I was doing was not right. Religion mattered to our people in a way that few other things did. I really should stop it, I told myself.

It took me another week to stop my miracle-making. I didn't want to end it abruptly, so I phased it out. My grandma was disappointed when ash stopped appearing in our prayer room. Crowds at our house dwindled. Things went back to normal. The episode became a family legend.

As I reflect on that episode now, I realize it taught me two important lessons.

Humans are keen to create and believe in our stories.





When something is too hard to comprehend, we resort to improbable explanations like divine interventions and magical occurrences. The unknown scares us, while such explanations comfort us.

The universe is complex, and we have just scratched the surface of knowing everything there is to know about it. When something comes into our consciousness that doesn't compute, the simplest thing is to ascribe it to a simplistic, unfalsifiable explanation like an unknowable God. As tempting as it is to accept this Occam's razor, it is important for us to resist it. Not only do such explanations take us away from truth, but they make us more gullible and more exploitable to people with their own agendas.

We should continue to look for reasonable explanations. We should teach our kids to think and question critically. We should teach them, and ourselves, to look for data, causal links, and theories that can be verified. This, I have come to believe, is one of best ways to keep pushing humanity forward.


Not being true to yourself and to others is a recipe for unhappiness.

The sole focus of my little experiment was to prove a point to myself. But the unintended consequence of it was that dozens of people believed wrongly that there was a miracle happening at my house. I had, effectively, misled them with little effort. My theory was proven right.

That fact, however, didn't bring me happiness. In fact, quite the opposite. The adrenaline rush faded quickly, and I was left with a guilt that gnawed at my innards like a hungry wolf. It took me a while to shake off the guilt. But just as a child that learns to not poke her finger in a vat of boiling milk, I learned that misleading others, even for an innocuous purpose and even if it doesn't harm anyone visibly, brings you no small amount of angst. You can argue I was doing that in service of truth, but in the process, I was not acting true.

Sometimes we betray truth without thinking that we are doing so. We do it whenever we pretend to be someone we are not, to be more liked or more successful. We do it when we profess a passion, a love, or an interest that we do not have. The lesson from my little miracle-making experiment applies in that case too. Not being true to yourself or to others may help you achieve your short-term goal, but in the long run, you will hurt yourself.

Use scientific thinking to seek truth. As you do that, be true to yourself and to others. As Polonius said to Hamlet:

"This above all: to thine own self be true

And it must follow, as the night the day

Thou canst not then be false to any man."
