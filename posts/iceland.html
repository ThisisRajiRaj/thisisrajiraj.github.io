--- layout: post title: Appreciate hot showers, and other life lessons date: Sep 16, 2021 ---

I once lived in an ancient house that had no running water. The kitchen was dark, cave-like, with cold stone floors. It had a dank corner with a drain set on the floor. My aunt washed vegetables, rice, plates and cups here.

At nights, this corner came alive. Out of nowhere, you'd hear a croak. Then more croaks, and you'd see little, slimy, brown frogs swarming out of the drain. Then came cockroaches that crawled, and then the creepiest of all, cockroaches that flew.

There were no bathrooms in the house too. To answer nature's calls, you had to draw water from a well and lug it to an "outhouse" in the backyard. In the outhouse, a single yellow bulb dangling from the ceiling, you had to stay alert for snakes and scorpions. You heated water on wood stoves to wash yourselves if you wanted hot water, your clothes and hair absorbing the smell of burning wood.

I barely registered any of these as problems or "lack" back then. All I wanted, as a little girl that lived with her aunt, was warmth and love. I didn't mind that there was no easy access to bathrooms or water. I just accepted what I had.

Likewise, today I accept other things without ever noticing. In my current reality, I do not have to wait in line or draw water from a well for drinking water. My kitchen, though never spotlessly clean despite my concerted attempts, is well-lit and warm. Other than the occasional spider, I have no critter in my bathrooms. Hot water gushes out of my shower the moment I turn it on. But I do not notice them or the comforts they offer. I do not stop to appreciate or feel grateful. Except when I travel….

I often travel with a single backpack over my back. Traveling with a single backpack means you have little more than your bare necessities with you. You learn to live minimally. On the other hand, when you do get access to more than those bare necessities, you take notice. And taking notice is half the battle toward appreciating.


Noticing is half the battle toward appreciating.


-

In August, I backpacked in Iceland for two weeks, thru-hiking the famous Laugavegur trail. I walked every day, my only concerns circling on what to eat, where to sleep, and when to wash up. I stayed at rustic mountain huts every night. I cooked meals in the hut kitchens using only what I had within my backpack. I slept on hard mattresses that were under 3-feet wide. I showered in hot water most days, but there was nothing cozy or warming about these showers. I got cold before I could step into my clothes again.

At the end of this 5-day adventure, I stayed in a hotel in Reykjavik where I could finally lay my head on soft, down pillows and sleep on a queen-size mattress covered in starched white sheets. I could shower in piping hot water and feel warm at the end of it. I could walk over to a restaurant and order myself a plate of spicy Pad Thai and sweet coconut milk tea. I noticed and appreciated every single luxury after my minimal existence on the trail.

This is one of the benefits of consciously stepping away: familiar things seem more delightful when you come back to them. I needed to step away this year for more than that feeling of appreciation. I needed a mental break after the many work commitments and the family and health crises of these last few months. I decided on traveling to Iceland because Iceland had a solid A on vaccination rates, and it is a small, spectacular country.

I learned a lot about Iceland in this trip. Firstly, that the country is breathtakingly beautiful. Secondly, that the land is as merciless as it is beautiful. The wind whips you about and the rain slashes your face. Iceland is on a hot spot where hot lava pours out of the earth, AND it straddles two tectonic plates. Just recently the country saw over 18000 earthquakes in a week and an erupting volcano. Thanks to this inhospitable habitat, much vegetation doesn't survive here natively. But the resourceful Icelandic people have found a way to thrive. They grow vegetables in greenhouses using their abundant geothermal energy. They roof their houses in corrugated metal to circumvent the lack of wood. People, like in other Scandinavian countries, are exceedingly trusting of others. Veganism is well understood and catered to, even though the native Icelandic cuisine has some "interesting" foods like fermented shark.



I learned more than these facts about Iceland, of course. Any journey where you step out of your normal life and disconnect can widen your soul, and this was one such journey.


-

Before I left for Iceland, I had - obviously - done a ton of research about hiking the Laugavegur and an extension to it called the Fimmvorduhals. Some of what I saw on YouTube and read online scared the guts out of me. Many people obsessed over the dangers of the river crossings, billed the exposed ridges as daunting. I worried that some sections of the trail were too technical for me. But I firmed up my resolve and set out on the journey anyway. As I walked, I realized how my experience could be very different from others - river crossings were refreshing to me, and the the sections I had worried about the most were not that scary. So my first lesson was to always seek the truth for myself and not let other people's fears and worries and experiences stop me. This was true for my hike, but it is equally true in work and life as well.

The first day on the Laugavegur trail was the steepest ascent of the trip. It was made further intense by the heavy fog that had descended on us and the cutting wind that pushed us about. The ascents were steep, parts with loose volcanic dirt and no purchase for the feet. Nikki, a woman I had befriended at the first hut, told me something that day that helped me throughout the journey and - I think - will help me in my life as well. Focus on one step at a time and just pay attention to where you put your feet - nothing else. The terrain ahead may be tricky to navigate, but if you keep your focus, look at where you are putting your feet, and keep calm, you will get through even the most challenging obstacles. Do not let your imaginations unrelated to just the few feet in front of you drain you.



As I trudged on, getting over tricky gullies and fording swift rivers, I began to feel more confident. That confidence in turn helped me ford swifter rivers and navigate trickier gullies. I thought about if I do that outside of my hikes, if I ever stopped to feel proud of my accomplishments and let them help me in my regular life. I realized that I could do more here: I could do better at taking mental note of my successes and using them to fuel the confidence needed for future successes.

On my last day on Iceland, I went to see a live volcano called Fagradalsfjall. The volcano had started erupting in March of this year and had been spewing sulfuric steam, ash, and molten magma ever since. I had never seen anything like it before - the viscous magma, its slow pace, the sizzle, the rising steam, the singeing heat that got hotter as you closed in. I stared at its destruction in awe, realizing the smallness of my worries, my expectations, my disappointments, my squabbles, my pet peeves, and my self-criticism.



Volcanoes are powerful, timeless forces that shape this planet. They annihilate everything in their path, and that annihilation lays the base for more creation. In the face of such limitless, endless cycles of this universe, I realized our petty affairs are transitory. Everything shall pass one day, our most traumatizing worries, our biggest embarrassments, our most intense crises, even our most beautiful works…

Hiking the Laugavegur and road tripping around Iceland gave me many wonderful experiences. Thinking back on it, I am reminded of the moment when a beloved character dies in the gripping Amazon Prime series "Vikings". She says: "Weep not…I have lived a full life." What does living a full life mean to me? What does it mean to you?

There is so much that this beautiful world offers that we can fill our lives with. At the end of my life, I hope I can look back and feel the way this beloved character felt about hers. That I have lived a full life.
