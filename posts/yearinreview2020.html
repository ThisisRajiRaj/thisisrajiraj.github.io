As 2020 dawned, I made a list of things I wanted to do this year.

  • Travel more
  • Write more
  • Read more
  • Watch less TV
  • Strength-train more at the gym
  • …and so on.

No surprise, but only a few of those came true. It was a case of the best laid plans of mice and men gone awry. I did write more and read more. But traveling came to a screeching halt. There was little if any gym. And boy, did I binge-watch TV! All those episodes of The Crown and Unorthodox and the many, many late-night comedy shows were rivers of grain for my grindstone.


2020 has been a year like no other in my recent memory.

I remember the day when we first learnt that this year was going to be different. It was early March, a cool, overcast day in Seattle. There was a sharp smell of fresh cut grass in the air. As I walked to my car at the end of a long workday, I looked at my phone, and there it was, at the top of my unread email - a message from our one of our VPs telling us to work from home until end of the month. All travel was suspended. My heart sank. I had a few trips and events I was looking forward to, including a keynote at a Codess event, and a visit to Lagos to see my team. I comforted myself that things will change soon, hopefully in April.

April came and went. Dense morning fogs began to give way to watercolor blue skies. I continued to work hard to find answers the many new questions of our new reality. Should I work with my laptop, perched on my sofa, or should I pony up the money for a proper office setup? How do I deal with my now-constant neck pain? Should I turn on the camera when my home was a mess, and my hair was an even bigger mess? Should I exercise in the morning or the evening? Whose turn was it to cook lunch? Why were these so much dust on tables and so many dishes in the sink? Should I buy more canned beans and soap? Should I be sanitizing the many cardboard boxes that got delivered from Amazon? Do I still fit into my jeans? How can I help my team feel connected and energized, when all around them was hardship, chaos, and unknowns? How can I make sure my 75-year-old dad and my 81-year-old mother-in-law were safe and cared for? What happens if one of them falls sick and I can't travel?


Slowly though, things started to fall into place.


I started feeling less anxious. I created new routines for myself. I started running in the morning, meditating in the evening, bookending my workdays. I started clearing dishes in the sink after work, while listening to audiobooks. I checked in with my team regularly. I started talking to our parents every day instead of just the weekends. These habits and new routines began to help.

I still missed seeing my people at work and traveling. But I started feeling less agitated about it all. I even began to appreciate the blessings this new reality bestowed on me. I don't have to spend so much time in traffic, feeling peeved about bad drivers. I don't have to attend Happy Hours where I pretended to enjoy the conversations when all I wanted to do was go home, get into my pajamas, listen to nothing but the low hum of my fridge, and lose myself in a book. I had so much more time for connecting with my family, with my husband. I had so much more time for my books.

Speaking of having copious time for books, I read a TON this year. Most book readers love to recommend books to others, and I am no exception. So here is a list of books that I selected for you from the books I read in 2020 (with a few pictures of "my year in review"). You might have read some of these, and you might want to check out some others. If either of these is true, I'd love to hear from you!


By far, the best book I read this year was Becoming by Michelle Obama.

Becoming is the story of Michelle Obama's growing up and her years in the White House. In it, we see Michelle, for the first time, as the human being that she is - her flaws, her anxieties, her strengths, and all. Not just the made-up character in her flowing gowns and J. Crew dresses, sometimes cast in unflattering light, and sometimes in glorifying light, by the media. In this book, she comes across as someone who has worked hard for her accomplishments, as someone who wants to make a difference in the world. I loved reading about her "box-checking", accomplishment-craving, perfectionist habits - habits that I battle with in myself.

Here is a quote that I underlined in my copy of Becoming that REALLY speaks to me.

It wasn't pretty, but I found it encouraging in the end, proof that I could work my way out of just about any hole. Whatever deficits I might have arrived with, coming from an inner-city high school, it seemed that I could make up for them by putting in extra time, asking for help when I needed it, and learning to pace myself and not procrastinate.


I later read her husband's new book "A Promised Land".

Barack Obama's A Promised Land is a much more expansive, if a much less personal, book. His book is a telling of grand history by an ex-President of a powerful country. We get to learn about the inner workings of politics, the details behind how some consequential decisions (like the sanctions against Iran, signing of the ACA, repeal of don't-ask-don't-tell) got made. We travel with him to Copenhagen, Moscow, and Mumbai. We learn from the wisdom of W.E.B Du Bois, Gandhi, and Martin Luther King Jr. We begin to appreciate the immensity of the work in front of the leaders of powerful countries. For all its merits, though, the book is dense, wearying, and hard to keep your focus on. At times, it feels like a stump speech. You question the honesty of the writing. And at times, you wish that the editor had been more draconian and cut the prose in half.

Michelle Obama's writing in Becoming, on the other hand, is intimate, crisp, honest, clear, and enjoyable - like a thick coat of melted butter on a piece of toast. Smooth, delicious, and decadent. I wish Michelle Obama would write another book - she is indeed a skilled writer!

I know some of you will be taking time off work this month. Holidays are a time to reflect and recharge. What better way to recharge than with a good book? And if there is just one book you can read from now until the end of the year, I recommend Becoming by Michelle Obama. I hope you enjoy it.
